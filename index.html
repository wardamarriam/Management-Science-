About The Course

What is Management Science?

Management Science is the use of structured, quantitative thinking — models, data, and logical frameworks — to make better decisions under real-world constraints like budgets, time, and uncertainty. Instead of guessing or relying purely on intuition, it gives decision-makers a clear way to define their goal, identify their limits, and find the choice that performs best within those limits. This semester, every project in this portfolio applied that same core idea to a different kind of problem.

Concepts Learned This Semester
🎯

Binary Optimization

Modelling "select it or don't" decisions using 0/1 variables, then maximizing an objective (like satisfaction or skill score) without breaking a budget constraint. Used in Projects 01 and 04.

📐

Model Components

Breaking any decision problem into five parts: the Set (your options), Parameters (known values), Decision Variables (your choices), the Objective Function (what to maximize), and the Constraint (your limits).

🌳

Decision Trees

Mapping out every possible decision, chance event, and outcome before acting — so that irreversible choices and their consequences are visible in advance. Used in Projects 02 and 05.

📊

Sensitivity Analysis

Testing how a model's optimal solution changes when one input changes — like a budget, a cost, or a score — to understand how reliable and stable a decision really is.

🔍

Systems Thinking

Looking beyond what's visible to identify the hidden, interconnected systems that keep something running — and asking what would break if one of those systems failed. Used in Project 03.

⚖️

Critical Evaluation of Tools

Learning that every model — including decision trees and optimization — has limitations: oversimplification, data requirements, and sensitivity to small input errors. Knowing when to combine tools.

Management Science · Budget Optimization

The Pizza Optimizer 🍕

Hands-on spreadsheet activity · Management Science

Binary VariablesObjective FunctionConstraintsSensitivity Analysis

Project Overview

This activity introduced the core structure of a Management Science model through a fun but mathematically real problem: choosing the best pizza toppings under a 7.00ww budget. Each topping has a known cost and satisfaction score, and the goal is to select a combination that maximizes total satisfaction without exceeding the budget.

What makes this a proper management science problem is its structure. The toppings are the set, their costs and scores are parameters, whether each topping is selected is a binary decision variable (1 = yes, 0 = no), the sum of satisfaction scores is the objective function to maximize, and the budget is the constraint. I built a spreadsheet model to test combinations and then performed sensitivity analysis to understand how the optimal solution shifts when inputs change.

This matters because the same logic — maximize an outcome subject to constraints using structured variables — underlies real-world decisions in production scheduling, resource allocation, and supply chain planning.

02
Management Science · Decision Analysis

Decision Tree Construction 🌳

Group activity · Scenario 2 — The Sleeping Dragon · Partner: Khadicha (2417758)

Decision TreeUncertaintyBranching LogicScenario Analysis

Project Overview

A decision tree is a Management Science tool that maps out every possible choice and its consequences in a structured, visual way — branching from an initial decision node through chance events and further decisions until every outcome is captured. It forces decision-makers to think ahead before acting, rather than reacting to problems as they arise.

In this activity, Khadicha and I were given Scenario 2 — The Sleeping Dragon: a kingdom trapped in endless winter, with a sleeping dragon beneath the northern mountains. The legends are split — the dragon may be causing the winter, or it may be suppressing something far more dangerous underground. The king's council must decide what to do. We built a full decision tree across five levels mapping all major choices: investigate, attack, leave, or work with the dragon — and the downstream consequences of each.

Our recommended path was to investigate first — send explorers and mages, study ancient texts, speak with northern tribes, and discover why the dragon is sleeping before committing to any irreversible action. This avoids unnecessary danger and ensures the kingdom makes an informed decision rather than acting blindly.

What Did I Learn?

Knowledge

A decision tree makes implicit reasoning explicit. By drawing every branch before deciding, we could see that attacking first closes off the "work with dragon" branch permanently — an irreversible move. Investigating first keeps all branches open. This taught me that in decision analysis, preserving optionality has real value.

Skills

I practised structuring a complex, ambiguous problem into decision nodes, chance events, and outcomes — the same structure used in business decisions involving uncertainty. Building the tree collaboratively also required us to agree on what counted as a branch versus a sub-outcome, which sharpened my logical thinking.

Reflection

The scenario was fictional, but the lesson was real: acting without information is almost always more costly than gathering it first. The "attack immediately" branch led to the worst outcomes in every sub-path we traced. I would apply this framework to any real decision involving uncertainty — from business strategy to personal choices.

Systems Thinking in Star Wars 🚀

Film analysis assignment · Star Wars Episode V: The Empire Strikes Back

Systems ThinkingLogisticsHidden SystemsManagement Science

Project Overview

Systems thinking is a Management Science lens that looks beyond what is visible on the surface to identify the interconnected processes and systems that make something work. Rather than seeing isolated events, systems thinking asks: what are all the components, feedback loops, and dependencies keeping this running?

For this assignment, I watched Star Wars Episode V: The Empire Strikes Back and chose the asteroid chase scene featuring Han Solo and the Millennium Falcon. On the surface, it looks like a dramatic action sequence — but beneath it, a complex set of hidden systems is operating. The Empire's TIE fighters and Star Destroyers are chasing the Falcon through a dangerous asteroid field, the Falcon is suffering technical failures mid-chase, and every ship involved depends on systems that are never shown on screen but are absolutely essential for them to keep flying and fighting.

I identified the ship repair and ship management system as the hidden system in this scene. This includes navigation, communication, damage control, tracking, and warning systems — all of which connect to the broader lens of Logistics and Management Science.

Management Science · Custom Optimization Problem

Workshop Schedule Optimizer 📅

Spreadsheet model · Google Sheets · Binary Linear Optimization

Binary VariablesObjective FunctionConstraintSensitivity Analysis

Project Overview

This is a custom optimization problem I designed and solved independently in Google Sheets. The scenario: a company needs to select the best training workshops for its interns, but is limited to a total of 20 training hours. Each workshop has a fixed number of hours and a skill score. The goal is to select which workshops to include so that the total skill score is maximized without exceeding the hour budget.

This is structurally identical to the Pizza Optimizer (Project 01), but built from scratch on a custom scenario — proving that the same Management Science model structure (binary variables, objective function, constraint) applies across completely different real-world problems. The model was built with SUMPRODUCT formulas in Google Sheets, with decision variables as a 0/1 column that I manually toggled to find the optimal solution.

I also conducted a sensitivity analysis — increasing the hour budget from 20 to 25 hours to see how the optimal selection and skill score changed.

Final Conclusion (from spreadsheet)

The objective was to maximize the total skill score while keeping total training hours at or below 20 hours. Using a binary linear optimization model, the optimal solution was to select Excel Analytics, Public Speaking, Project Management, Data Visualization, and Negotiation Skills. The total training hours used were 20 and the total skill score achieved was 78. Increasing the budget to 25 hours produced a new optimal schedule (Excel Analytics, Public Speaking, Project Management, Data Visualization, AI Fundamentals) using 24 hours with a skill score of 96.

What Did I Learn?

Knowledge

I learned that the same binary optimization structure from the Pizza Optimizer applies to completely different real-world problems. AI Fundamentals had the highest raw skill score (28) but was excluded in the base model because its 7-hour cost was too high given the budget — score-per-hour efficiency determines the optimal selection, not raw score alone.

Skills

I designed this model entirely from scratch — creating the scenario, defining the data, writing the SUMPRODUCT formulas, and building the sensitivity analysis in a separate sheet. This was the first time I built a full Management Science model independently without a template.

Reflection

The sensitivity analysis was the most interesting part. Adding just 5 more hours shifted the optimal solution — AI Fundamentals entered and Negotiation Skills dropped out. This shows that resource constraints don't just limit outcomes, they reshape which choices are optimal entirely. The same principle applies to budgets, staffing, and any constrained resource in business.

Decision Trees — Theory & Limitations 🌲

Cornell-style notes · Reading-based project

Decision TheoryUncertaintyLimitationsReal-World Application

Project Overview

This project is based on a reading and note-taking exercise focused on decision trees as a theoretical tool in Management Science. While Project 02 involved building a decision tree by hand for a specific scenario, this project goes deeper into the conceptual side: how decision trees work as a general method, where they are used in the real world, and — critically — where they fall short.

My notes follow a Cornell format with three layers: the top addresses real-world and classroom application, the middle addresses limitations of decision trees for decision-making, and the bottom captures my own questions and points of confusion after reading. This structure builds not just knowledge but critical thinking — asking what a tool cannot do is just as important as knowing what it can.

Decision trees are used across industries — from finance (should we invest?) to medicine (which treatment?) to management strategy (should we expand?) — because they make complex, branching decisions visible and comparable before any action is taken.

What Did I Learn?

Knowledge

Decision trees are powerful for structured, sequential decisions under uncertainty — but their reliability depends entirely on the quality of input data and probabilities. A small change in an estimated probability can flip the optimal decision completely. This is why sensitivity analysis (Project 04) is so important alongside any optimization model.

Skills

This reading project developed my ability to critically evaluate a tool rather than just use it. I practised Cornell note-taking as a structured reading method, and then extended my thinking by researching alternatives and comparing their strengths and limitations — a skill directly useful for any evidence-based decision in business.

Reflection

My biggest question going in was: can decision trees be combined with other methods? The answer is yes — sensitivity analysis can test the robustness of a tree's outputs, and simulation can fill gaps where probabilities are uncertain. This portfolio itself is a demonstration: Projects 01, 02, and 04 use optimization; Projects 02 and 05 use decision trees; Project 03 uses systems thinking. Real management science combines all of them.
