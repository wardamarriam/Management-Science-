What is Management Science?

An introduction to the discipline this portfolio is built around — and what I studied during Semester 5 under Instructor Joshua Dean.

Management Science — A Scientific Approach to Decision-Making

Management Science is the discipline of applying mathematical models, quantitative analysis, and structured thinking to help organizations make better decisions. Rather than relying on intuition alone, management science translates real-world problems into formal models — defining what you control (decision variables), what you know (parameters), what you want to achieve (objective function), and what limits you (constraints). The optimal solution is then found through systematic analysis.

The field draws on mathematics, statistics, computer science, and economics. It is used in supply chain management, healthcare planning, financial optimization, logistics, scheduling, and anywhere else complex decisions need to be made under uncertainty or resource constraints.

Why It Matters

Every organization — a hospital, an airline, a warehouse, a government — faces decisions with limited resources and competing objectives. Management science gives a rigorous, repeatable process for finding the best possible answer, rather than guessing. Even small improvements in resource allocation can save millions of dollars or hours at scale.

How It Connects to This Portfolio

Every project in this portfolio is an application of management science thinking: building a model, identifying constraints, optimizing an objective, and analyzing how the solution responds to changing inputs. The pizza, the workshops, the decision tree, and the Star Wars scene are all examples of the same underlying discipline.

Project 01 — Optimization

Branch & Bound

How do you pick the best pizza toppings on a tight budget — without checking every possible combination?

Section 1

Project Overview

This project grew out of a class assignment where we applied the Branch and Bound (B&B) algorithm to a real optimization problem: choosing pizza toppings that maximize a "deliciousness score" without exceeding a $7 budget.

Branch and Bound is a method for solving integer programming problems — decisions that can only be Yes or No (0 or 1), not fractions. It works by systematically exploring a decision tree, but pruning branches that mathematically cannot beat the best solution found so far. This makes it far faster than brute-force search.

The concept matters because real-world decisions — staffing, routing, resource allocation — are full of yes/no choices with budget constraints. B&B is one of the core tools that makes those problems solvable at scale.

Knowledge

Project 02 — Binary Linear Programming

Workshop Schedule Optimization

Which training workshops should an intern attend to maximize skill development — without exceeding a 20-hour time budget?

Section 1

Project Overview

This project is a custom optimization problem I designed and solved using Binary Linear Programming (BLP). The scenario: an intern must choose from 8 available workshops, each with a time cost (hours) and a skill-score value. The goal is to maximize total skill score without exceeding a 20-hour training budget.

Unlike continuous LP, every decision here is binary — a workshop is either attended (1) or skipped (0). This makes it an integer programming problem, which is harder to solve but reflects how most real decisions actually work: you can't attend half a workshop.

I formulated the model, set up the spreadsheet in Excel with a SUMPRODUCT objective, added the hour constraint, and solved it with Solver. I then ran a sensitivity analysis by increasing the available hours from 20 to 25 to see how the optimal schedule would change.

Project 03 — Decision Analysis

Decision Trees & The Sleeping Dragon

A kingdom in endless winter. A sleeping dragon. Five possible paths forward. How do you map uncertainty into a structured decision?

Section 1

Project Overview

This project comes from a group activity where we were given a scenario — The Sleeping Dragon — and asked to build a decision tree mapping out every possible course of action a kingdom could take, along with the consequences of each path.

A decision tree is a visual tool that structures multi-stage decisions. Each node represents a choice or event; each branch represents one possible outcome. Following a path from root to leaf traces one complete sequence of decisions and their result. The tree makes trade-offs visible and forces you to think through consequences before acting.

The scenario was fictional, but the skill is entirely real: decision trees are used everywhere from medical diagnosis to supply chain risk planning to machine learning classification algorithms.

What Did I Learn?

Knowledge

A decision tree maps multi-stage choices as a graph. Each path from root to leaf is a complete scenario. The tree forces you to think ahead: what happens after this choice leads to that outcome?

Project 04 — Management Science Fundamentals

The Pizza Optimizer 🍕

Learning the core vocabulary and structure of Management Science — through the surprisingly rich problem of choosing pizza toppings on a 7ww budget.

Section 1

Project Overview

This activity introduced the foundational vocabulary and method of Management Science — the discipline of using quantitative models and mathematical analysis to make better decisions. Rather than abstract theory, every concept was grounded in a concrete problem: choosing pizza toppings within a 7ww budget to maximize satisfaction.

The scenario forces you to define a set, identify parameters, choose decision variables, write an objective function, and respect a constraint — the complete skeleton of any management science model. What makes this project distinct from Project 1 (Branch & Bound) is its focus on recognizing and naming model structure before solving.

Below, each key term is shown alongside my own answer from the activity.

Set

A collection of distinct items the decisions apply to.

My answer: The 10 available pizza toppings — Buffalo mozzarella through Fried eggplant.

Parameters

Known, fixed values in the model — not things you decide.

My answer: Unit cost and satisfaction score for each topping, plus the 7.00ww budget. These were given, not chosen.

Decision Variable

The value you control to make the decision.

My answer: Binary variable for each topping — 0 = not selected, 1 = selected.

Objective Function

The formula measuring what you maximize or minimize.

My answer: Maximize total satisfaction = SUM of (score × selected) for all toppings. SUM of column F in the spreadsheet.

Constraint

A rule the solution must not violate.

My answer: Total cost ≤ 7.00ww. SUM of column E must not exceed 7.00.

Optimal Solution

The feasible solution with the best objective value.

My answer: Salame + Ricotta + 'Nduja + Zucchini + Fried eggplant → score 26.0, cost 6.50ww.

Sensitivity Analysis

Testing how the optimal solution responds to changes in inputs.

My answer: Salame stops being worth it at 3.00ww. Fried eggplant drops out at score 3.5. Buffalo mozzarella enters at score 5.3.

Binary Variable

A decision variable that can only be 1 (yes) or 0 (no).

My answer: Each xᵢ ∈ {0,1} — you either take a topping or you don't; no fractions allowed.

What Did I Learn?

Knowledge

Management science breaks any decision into five parts: set, parameters, decision variables, objective function, and constraints. Identifying these five things is the first step to solving any optimization problem.

Score/Cost Ratio Insight

Without the model, intuition picks Parma ham (score 7.2 — looks great). The model shows it costs 3ww for a ratio of 2.40 — one of the worst options. Quantitative analysis corrects what gut feeling gets wrong.

Project 05 — Systems Thinking

Hidden Systems in The Empire Strikes Back

What does an asteroid chase scene reveal about logistics, ship management, and the invisible systems that keep complex operations running?

⭐ Star Wars: Episode V · Asteroid Field Scene

Section 1

Project Overview

This project came from an assignment where we watched a scene from a film and applied a Systems Thinking lens to identify the hidden systems operating in the background. I chose the asteroid chase scene from Star Wars: Episode V — The Empire Strikes Back, where Han Solo pilots the Millennium Falcon through a deadly asteroid field to escape Imperial forces.

Systems Thinking is a management science approach that looks beyond individual events to understand the underlying systems, feedback loops, and interdependencies that produce them. Instead of asking "what happened?", it asks "what system made this happen — and what keeps it running?"

On the surface it's an exciting chase sequence. Through a systems thinking lens, it becomes a study in logistics — ship repair, navigation, communications, tracking, and warning systems all operating simultaneously under extreme stress. The scene reveals that every complex operation depends on invisible infrastructure that most observers never notice.

Real-World Application

Amazon's fulfilment centres, hospital emergency departments, airline operations — all face the same challenge: multiple systems must work simultaneously under stress, with no downtime allowed. Systems thinking is how managers design and fix them.
